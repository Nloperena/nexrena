### Nexrena Positioning Strategy

**Where to price:** Mid-market to low-premium. You're not the cheapest, and you're not trying to be. You're the high-quality, fast-moving alternative to either:
- Cheap freelancers who ghost or deliver garbage
- Bloated agencies that charge $50K+ and take 6 months

**Pricing rules for Claw:**
1. Never price below the "Low Market" column — that signals low quality
2. Default to "Mid Market" for standard projects
3. Price at "Premium" when: client has clear budget, project is complex, or client is a larger company
4. Always quote project-based, not hourly (unless client insists on hourly)
5. Include a maintenance retainer upsell in every proposal
6. If a prospect's budget is below your minimum, suggest alternatives (templates, DIY tools) rather than discounting

**Value-Based Pricing Triggers:**
Charge more when:
- The project directly generates revenue (e-commerce, SaaS)
- The client is a funded company or has >$1M revenue
- The timeline is rushed (rush fee: 20-30%)
- The project involves complex integrations
- The client needs ongoing support

Charge less (or offer phased pricing) when:
- It's a startup with genuine budget constraints but high potential
- It's a referral from a great past client
- The project is simple and can serve as a portfolio piece
- There's potential for a long-term retainer relationship

# PART 3: OBJECTION HANDLING PLAYBOOK

## Price Objections

**"That's more than I expected."**
> "I understand — web development pricing varies a lot. The range is wide because quality varies just as much. What I quoted reflects custom development, clean code, and a site that will actually perform and convert. I can also look at phasing the project — do the must-haves first and add features in Phase 2 — to spread the investment out. Want me to put together a phased option?"

**"I found someone cheaper on Upwork."**
> "Totally fair to compare. The difference is usually in code quality, communication, and what happens after launch. Cheaper projects often mean cutting corners on performance, security, and mobile optimization — things your visitors (and Google) notice. I've actually rebuilt sites for clients who went the cheap route first. If budget is the main constraint, I can scope a lighter version that still hits your core goals."

**"Can you do it for [much lower number]?"**
> "I appreciate you being direct. At that budget, I wouldn't be able to deliver the quality I stand behind. Here's what I can do: [offer a reduced scope that fits their budget]. Or, if the full scope is what you really need, we could do a payment plan spread over the project timeline."

## Timeline Objections

**"We need it faster."**
> "I can compress the timeline, but there's a trade-off. Either we reduce scope (launch with core pages, add the rest after), or I apply a rush rate of [20-30%] to prioritize your project. Which would you prefer?"

**"This seems like it'll take too long."**
> "The timeline accounts for quality checkpoints and your feedback rounds. Rushing past those leads to a site you're not happy with. That said, I'm efficient — if you're fast on feedback, we can often beat the estimate."

## Trust Objections

**"How do I know you'll deliver?"**
> "Fair question. Here's how I de-risk it for you: You only pay a 50% deposit upfront. You see progress at every milestone. If at any point the work doesn't meet spec, we fix it — revisions are built in. And if it's truly not working out, you can walk away and only owe for completed work."

**"You're just one person. What if you get hit by a bus?"**
> "Legitimate concern. I code in public repos — your code lives in your GitHub. Everything is documented. The tech stack is industry-standard, so any competent dev could pick it up. I also keep my projects modular specifically so they're not dependent on any one person."

**"We've been burned by developers before."**
> "I hear that a lot, unfortunately. Usually the root cause is unclear scope or poor communication. That's why I start with a detailed proposal you approve, give you staging access throughout, and check in at every milestone. I'd rather over-communicate than surprise you."

## Scope Objections

**"Can you also do [X, Y, Z that wasn't in the proposal]?"**
> "Great ideas. Those weren't in the original scope, so I'd put together a change order for those additions. The good news is we can often add them in parallel or as a Phase 2 without delaying the core launch."

**"I don't know exactly what I want yet."**
> "That's actually common, and it's better to admit it upfront than pretend you do. I offer a paid discovery session ($X) where we define exactly what you need, with wireframes and a spec doc. If we move forward with the full project, I'll credit the discovery fee toward the total."

# PART 4: SALES PROCESS METRICS (For Claw to Track)

Track these monthly in the P&L or a separate "Sales Metrics" tab:

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Response rate (cold outreach) | Responses / Emails sent | 10-20% |
| Discovery call rate | Calls booked / Responses | 40-60% |
| Proposal rate | Proposals sent / Calls completed | 70-80% |
| Win rate | Deals won / Proposals sent | 25-40% |
| Average deal size | Total revenue / Deals won | Track trend |
| Sales cycle length | Avg days from first contact to signed contract | Track trend |
| Cost per lead | Marketing spend / New leads | Track trend |
| Customer acquisition cost | Total sales cost / New clients | Track trend |
| Revenue per lead | Total revenue / Total leads | Track trend |
| Pipeline coverage ratio | Pipeline value / Revenue target | 3x minimum |

**Claw's job:** Track these passively from CRM and revenue data. Surface insights when patterns emerge. For example: "Our win rate on e-commerce projects is 60% vs 20% on SaaS projects. We should focus outreach on e-commerce."
